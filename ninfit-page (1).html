What is NinFit

Not a gym class.
A progression system.

NinFit is Elemental 5's core program — the foundation of everything we build. It trains parkour, tumbling, and trampoline together inside a structured level system, grounded in character development through the Five Elements framework.

Athletes work through 9 levels across five elemental phases. There's no rushing and no shortcutting — skills are signed off by coaches when they see consistent mastery in class. The progression is real because the standard is real.

  • Three disciplines, one programParkour, tumbling, and trampoline are trained and assessed together at every level. You develop across all three simultaneously.
  • Skills earned in class, not on test dayCoaches observe and sign off when they see mastery happening naturally. There's no pressure performance — just consistent training.
  • Character is part of the curriculumEvery class closes with a mat chat — intentional conversation built around the Five Elements character framework. Movement is the vehicle. Development is the destination.
  • 9 levels. Years of real growth.NinFit goes from foundational movement all the way through elite performance. There's always a next level worth working toward.

How Progression Works

The 8-Week Training Cycle

NinFit runs on a structured 8-week training arc. It's a curriculum rhythm — not a test countdown. Every week has a defined focus. Every session builds on the last. Any athlete can enter at any point and get a complete experience.

01
Train consistently

Every class follows the same structure — a focused 5-minute skill block, then rotation through all training zones. Coaches know what they're running. Athletes know what to expect. Consistency is the whole point.

02
Get skills signed off

Athletes carry a personal booklet with the anchor skills for their current level. Coaches sign off when they observe consistent mastery in class — not when an athlete performs on demand. There's no test day for Levels 1 through 3.

03
Level up naturally

When a coach sees everything signed off, the conversation happens. Leveling up isn't an event — it's a recognition. The booklet proves the work was done. The skills prove the athlete is ready.

Levels 1 → 2 → 3: milestone checkpoints, not formal tests
04
The one real gate

Level 3 to Level 4 is the only formal assessment in the program. It's a different class hour, a significantly higher skill threshold, and a genuine commitment shift. This one is scheduled, announced, and earned in full.

Levels 4 – 6

Everything changes
at Level 4.

Levels 1–3 are about building a body that can move. Level 4 is where that body starts being used with intention. The skills are harder, the coaching is sharper, and for the first time — competition is on the table.

The Level 3 to Level 4 gate is the only formal assessment in the NinFit program. Athletes who pass it don't just move to a new level — they move to a new class hour, a new standard, and a new identity as a competitive athlete.

Levels 4–6 are the pipeline to RMPC, the Rocky Mountain Parkour Championships — Colorado's premier youth parkour competition league — and beyond that, the US Parkour National Championships.

  • One gate. No exceptions.Athletes must pass a formal Level 3→4 assessment to enter this class. The standard is real, and so is the recognition when it's met.
  • Competition-oriented trainingThe four parkour zones — wall, floor, vault, bar — are trained with competition scoring in mind. Execution, flow, versatility, and difficulty become coaching targets.

The Competition Path

Where this leads.

E5's Intermediate program is built around a clear competitive pathway — from local Colorado competitions all the way to the national stage and beyond.

RMPC
Colorado Springs, CO
Rocky Mountain Parkour Championships

Colorado's first and largest youth parkour league. Athletes compete in Speed, Freestyle, and Tag across three age brackets — U9, U12, and U16. Freestyle is scored on Execution, Flow, Versatility, and Difficulty out of 40 points. This is E5's primary competitive target for Level 4–6 athletes.

US Parkour National Championships

The national governing body for parkour in the USA. Athletes compete in a regional qualifier circuit — Speed, Skill, and Style — with top finishers advancing to the USPK National Championships. E5's own coach George Mossman competed at the 2025 USPK Nationals at Tumble Tech in Texas.

How Competition Works

Training for
a real standard.

RMPC Freestyle is scored across four categories out of 40 points. Every category maps directly onto how E5 trains parkour — which means athletes who have been training in NinFit since Level 1 are already building the right habits for competition.

At Levels 4–6 the coaching becomes deliberate about these categories. It's no longer just "did you land it" — it's how cleanly, how fluidly, how varied, and how ambitious.

How E5 trains for this

Wall, floor, vault, and bar — E5's four parkour zones — map directly onto RMPC's Versatility scoring categories. Athletes who rotate through all four zones every class are naturally building the variety judges reward.

Common Questions

What parents ask

How does my child level up?

Coaches observe athletes in class every week and sign off on anchor skills when they see consistent mastery — not a single good rep on a good day, but the skill showing up naturally and reliably. When everything in the booklet is signed off, the conversation happens. No test, no countdown, no pressure performance.

What happens at Level 3 to 4?

Level 3 to Level 4 is the only formal assessment in the program. It involves a new class hour, a significantly higher skill standard, and a genuine commitment to the next level of training. When a Level 3 athlete has everything signed off, a formal assessment is scheduled. It's earned — and it means something.

My child is 6. Will they be behind the older kids?

Younger athletes (ages 5–7) start in Junior Ninjas and train alongside older NinFit students. They work through a foundation passport at their own pace — and when they've mastered those skills, they earn their NinFit booklet and enter the level system. It's not about age. It's about readiness.

What does a typical class look like?

Classes run in two blocks — parkour and tumbling/trampoline — each about 20 minutes. Every block starts with a structured 5-minute focus drill, then opens into rotation through training zones. The curriculum has a defined 8-week rhythm so athletes always know what to expect. Every class closes with a mat chat built around the Five Elements character framework.

Does my child need prior experience?

Level 1 is built for beginners. The skills start from the ground up — literally. If your child can follow directions and wants to move, they're ready for NinFit.

How long does it take to reach Level 4?

For a student training once per week, the realistic timeline is about a year of consistent training through Levels 1–3. The 8-week training cycle creates the rhythm — athletes who show up every week and engage fully will progress naturally. There's no rush and no shortcut. That's by design.

What's the difference between Levels 1–3 and Levels 4–6?

Levels 1–3 are developmental and foundation-focused. The Level 3→4 gate is the only formal assessment in the program. Starting at Level 4, training becomes competition-oriented. Execution, flow, versatility, and difficulty are deliberately coached. It's a real shift in focus and class hour.

What is Surge?

Surge is E5's dedicated competitive team for athletes who want to compete at RMPC (Rocky Mountain Parkour Championships) and national-level competitions. It's a small, coach-selected group that launches with the new facility. To be considered, athletes must be at Level 4 minimum and show serious commitment to competition training.

Can my child compete after Level 3?

Level 4 is where competitive training officially begins. That said, competitive readiness isn't just about level — it's about maturity, engagement, and desire. Athletes at Level 4+ who want to compete can pursue RMPC and regional competitions. Surge is the next step for those with serious competitive ambitions.
